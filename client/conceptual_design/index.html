Conceptual Design

Concept Statement

The UMD Mental Health Assistance System will replace the old behavioral health services provided by our client: the University of Maryland Health Center. It will provide 24-hour-a-day services to general students, especially to support freshmen students seeking mental health assistance. This service includes online mental health assistance for those who want to have an anonymous setting, for those seeking other resources, and for those who would like to meet outside of the UMD Health Center. The new system includes VR meetings, chatting, scheduled, although short notice, meetings outside of the health center, and video calls for those who live away from the campus and can’t commute to get assistance. The new design will be more inclusive of disabled students who wish to seek mental health treatment; our design will include various features, for example, a video with clear audio and subtitles to discuss treatment methods and processes to provide clear guidance. Furthermore, to acknowledge students of our existence, we will send weekly emails to students to seek our services if they are suffering from mental health disorders. Overall, previous behavioral health services failed to provide a variety of options, resulting in extensive waiting time and avoidance in seeking treatment. Compared to behavioral health services, our system will provide a variety of options to encourage students to seek mental health assistance comfortably. We will implement a UX solution that guides and encourages our users, primarily freshmen students, who experience mental health issues to seek our system. The changes we’ve made are minor. We made changes to some terminology to be more inclusive of those who need mental health assistance. In an earlier version, we were too narrow in our scope of the user by stating it was for those with mental health disorders, but now we are broadening our scope to include any student who needs mental health assistance with our continued focus on freshmen.

Storyboards

Our group split up the making of the 3 storyboards. We discussed the three different scenarios we were going to represent with our storyboards and split up the work.

We’ve chosen these three storyboards to tell how our system provides different features to help those who are in different situations. The first storyboard shows a student who suffers from mental instability but doesn’t have time to seek treatment due to an overload of schoolwork. Without having to go to the UMD health center, the student was able to talk to health professionals about his mental instability through a 24-hotline. The second storyboard shows a disabled student who finds it difficult to obtain assistance from the health faculty. By using the V.R chat option, now the student could talk to a mental health specialist without having to go to the health center. The third storyboard shows a student who has problems with social interaction. Without having to have face-to-face counseling, he can talk to health professionals through anonymous chat services. These differences in storyboards are extremely important because they show inclusive design capabilities to serve everyone regardless of gender, physical limitations, or preferences.

The V.R chat options and the demonstration of the user 24-hotline chat where the user can seek therapy are two ecological aspects that have made it into the storyboard. The reason the V.R chat and 24-hotline chat have an ecological perspective is because it shows how the user and health professional chat over a network using their own device. From an interaction standpoint, we can see in the storyboard that the student used the 24-hotline when he was anxious and didn't have time to physically go to the health center, so he engaged with his phone to call the 24-hotline. In another storyline, a crippled individual was unable to walk all the way to the health facility for counseling, so he used the virtual reality headset to receive his treatment.

Our group considered the user’s mental model by going through the information that was collected through our interviews and culminated into the personas we created. This information showed us whether our user’s mental model came from solely one perspective (ecological, interaction, emotional) or if it was a mixture of all three. The difference between the user’s mental model and the designer's mental model is mainly that the user’s mental model centered around the experience of seeking mental health, while the designer’s mental model centered on the features/services that would deliver those experiences. With the user’s mental model in hand, we were able to cater our initial plans for the storyboards to address the unique needs of our users through our created personas such as social interaction, disability, and schedule constraints.
